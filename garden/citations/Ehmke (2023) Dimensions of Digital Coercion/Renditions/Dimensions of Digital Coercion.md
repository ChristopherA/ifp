# Dimensions of Digital Coercion

**authored_by::[[Coraline Ada Ehmke]]**

_Clipped from [Ethical Source](https://ethicalsource.dev/publications/digital-coercion) on 2026-03-02_

_is_a::[[web_clipping]]; has_status::[[curated]]_

---

Online environments employ coercive control mechanisms across four dimensions:

### Attention Coercion

Demands excessive time and cognitive engagement beyond what users would willingly consent to. Tactics include notification systems, manufactured outrage, and infinite scroll — all designed to capture attention as an extractable resource.

### Ergonomic Coercion

Forces trade-offs between autonomy, privacy, and usability through design choices that restrict access to basic features. Users must surrender privacy or accept reduced functionality — a choice designed to look like consent.

### Trust Coercion

Requests assumption of benign intent without accountability. Exploits pressure to conform to dominant trust models, treating skepticism as unreasonable rather than addressing structural power imbalances.

### Cultural Coercion

Imposes Western or dominant cultural norms as participation prerequisites, marginalizing communities whose values, communication styles, or governance models differ from the defaults encoded in platforms.

### Application

The framework applies across commercial platforms (Google, Amazon, Meta) and open-source ecosystems alike. Coercion exists in both proprietary and open systems — the mechanisms differ but the patterns persist. Addressing digital oppression requires strategies that transcend the proprietary/open divide.
