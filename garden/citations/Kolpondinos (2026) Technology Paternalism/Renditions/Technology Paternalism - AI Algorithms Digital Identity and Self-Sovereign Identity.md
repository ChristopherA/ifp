# Technology Paternalism: AI, Algorithms, Digital Identity, and the Role of Self-Sovereign Identity

_Clipped from [KosmaConnect](https://www.kosmaconnect.net/interactionblog/technologypaternalism) on 2026-03-17_

---

## How It Starts

This article examines an anti-pattern increasingly shaping everyday interactions: decisions about access, visibility, and possibility embedded into systems we depend on, often justified as convenience, efficiency, or safety. By naming this dynamic as Technology Paternalism, the article analyzes how decision-making built into systems shapes choices before people actively make them and limits how they can be challenged.

## A Familiar Moment

The opening scenario depicts downloading a mobile app where a "quick setup" button appears prominent and reassuring, while an "advanced" alternative is labeled in discouraging terms. The user hasn't been forced — yet the structure of choices predetermines outcomes.

## What Is Paternalism?

Paternalism describes "interference with a person's autonomy — without their consent — justified by the belief that doing so is for their own good." Power asymmetry distinguishes paternalism from mere patronizing tone; it concerns who holds decision-making authority.

## What Is Technology Paternalism?

Technology Paternalism implements paternalism through technical systems. Spiekermann and Pallas (2006) raised concerns about ubiquitous computing restricting user behavior in ways users cannot challenge, introducing the concept of "the right to the last word."

Today, Technology Paternalism describes systems that shape, restrict, or pre-decide choices — justified as safety, efficiency, or protection. While appearing neutral, systems encode human, organizational, and institutional judgments through design, data, and governance.

## Four Forms of Technology Paternalism

### I — Design Paternalism

Works through defaults, layout, friction, and language around choices. Interface design systematically influences decisions while formally preserving choice — "dark patterns" that nudge users toward platform-serving outcomes. Examples include prominent "quick setup" buttons versus discouraged "advanced" options, and non-negotiable terms bundling data collection decisions users cannot modify.

### II — Algorithmic Paternalism

Occurs when automated systems decide what information or actions are appropriate without user input. Recommender systems rank, filter, and pre-select content before users see it. The risk involves progressively narrowing exposure — what Eli Pariser called a "filter bubble." Research shows asymmetry of effort: defaults require no action while seeking diversity requires deliberate work.

### III — Infrastructural Paternalism

Happens when systems become so embedded that participation is formally voluntary but practically unavoidable — refusal means losing access to dependent services. When credentials, reputation, and relationships accumulate within proprietary ecosystems, switching means losing recognized standing. The OECD documents how data portability and interoperability affect competition and lock-in.

The EU Data Act (applicable since September 2025) includes provisions enabling service switching, acknowledging structural dependency as requiring policy response. However, barriers often remain invisible — systems requiring specific devices or proprietary formats maintain dependency by narrowing viable alternatives.

### IV — Protective Paternalism

Restrictions justified through safety, security, or harm prevention language. This most socially accepted form becomes most easily overlooked. Mechanisms include blocked websites, intercepted communications, and centralized identity gatekeeping. Restrictions framed as protecting people from harm become difficult to contest — questioning them risks appearing irresponsible.

## When You're Not There: Agentic AI and Digital Identity

**Agentic AI** raises three questions: Was delegation genuinely your choice, or configured by platforms/employers? If something goes wrong, can you understand what happened? Can you override the agent's decision?

A 2026 Cloud Security Alliance survey found 84% of organizations doubted passing compliance audits on agent behavior. Only 21% maintain real-time agent registries, and fewer than a third reliably trace actions across environments. Deloitte's 2026 report showed while agentic AI adoption is expected to grow from 23% to 74% within two years, only 21% have mature governance in place.

**Digital Identity** systems verify credentials, validate eligibility, and determine recognition in healthcare, employment, borders, and civic participation. Questions include: Do you know applied criteria? If rejected, can you learn why? Can you contest outcomes?

When decisions embed in automated systems, people rarely know what criteria applied or how to challenge results. Miscategorization impacts those least able to push back.

**Combined Effect:** When AI agents access identity-verification systems, automated decisions multiply without traceable human accountability. The EU AI Act requires human intervention in high-risk systems affecting healthcare, services, employment, and critical infrastructure.

## Closing Thought

Technology Paternalism doesn't require bad intentions — only decisions embedded before anyone questions them and systems becoming infrastructure before anyone challenges them.

Four concrete capabilities counter this pattern:

1. The ability to override a system's decision
2. The ability to contest a system's decision
3. The ability to inspect reasoning behind decisions
4. A practical way to leave the system

## Preventing Coercion — New Self-Sovereign Identity Lenses

The Revisiting Self-Sovereign Identity initiative develops four coercion-prevention lenses aligned with Technology Paternalism forms:

- **Coercion Resistance:** Addresses dark patterns, profiling, lock-in, and internalized surveillance
- **Self-Coercion:** Examines invisible coercion through chilling effects and anticipatory compliance
- **Choice Architecture and Exit Rights:** How credentials, reputation, and biometrics trap people; why reversible design matters
- **Binding Commitments:** Distinguishing productive voluntary constraint from exploitative lock-in

"How ready are institutions and organizations for technology solutions that trust individuals as much as individuals are asked to trust them?"
